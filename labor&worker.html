His book, "The Problem of the Rupee: Its Origin and Its Solution" (1923), was a landmark study on Indian currency and monetary policy. This work provided a detailed analysis of India's financial issues under British rule and offered solutions for a stable and independent economic system. His recommendations were later used as a foundation for the establishment of the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) in 1935.

Ambedkar advocated for a centralized banking system to regulate currency, control inflation, and ensure financial stability. He emphasized the need for an independent monetary policy, a structured banking framework, and credit facilities for weaker sections of society. His ideas were incorporated into the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934, which led to the formation of the RBI as India's central bank.

Beyond RBI guidelines, Ambedkar was deeply concerned with economic planning and financial inclusion. He advocated for state-led industrialization, labor rights, and economic policies that would benefit the poor and marginalized communities. His vision ensured that India had a strong financial foundation post-independence, allowing for economic growth and monetary stability.
