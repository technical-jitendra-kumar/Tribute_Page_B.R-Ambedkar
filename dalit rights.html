Ambedkar was a relentless champion of social justice and Dalit rights, dedicating his life to the eradication of caste-based discrimination in India. Born into an untouchable (Dalit) family, he personally experienced the injustices of the caste system, which fueled his lifelong struggle for equality, dignity, and human rights for the oppressed. Through his writings, activism, and political leadership, he fought against the deep-rooted social evils of untouchability, caste oppression, and discrimination.

One of his most significant contributions was the Mahad Satyagraha of 1927, where he led Dalits to assert their right to drink water from public tanks, challenging caste-based restrictions. His relentless advocacy led to legal reforms that abolished untouchability and ensured reservations (affirmative action) in education, employment, and government institutions for Scheduled Castes (SCs) and Scheduled Tribes (STs).

Ambedkar also worked extensively to uplift Dalit women, advocating for education, equal rights, and social empowerment. He believed that true democracy could only be achieved when every individual, regardless of caste or gender, had access to opportunities and dignity. His efforts culminated in the inclusion of fundamental rights and social justice provisions in the Indian Constitution, ensuring protection for marginalized communities.

To escape caste oppression, he led the Dalit Buddhist Movement in 1956, inspiring millions to convert to Buddhism, embracing a life of equality and self-respect.
