He argues that while linguistic states could help in better governance and administration, they also pose risks of regionalism, communalism, and division if not handled properly.

Ambedkar supports the idea of linguistic states as a means to ensure efficient administration and protect cultural identity. However, he warns against excessive linguistic fanaticism that could weaken national unity. He proposes a balanced approach, suggesting that linguistic states should be formed with a strong central government to maintain national integrity. He also advocates for smaller states rather than large ones to prevent the dominance of any particular linguistic or regional group.

One of his most radical suggestions was to make Hindi the national language while ensuring English remains an associate language for effective communication. He feared that linguistic divisions might deepen social and economic inequalities, and therefore, he emphasized the need for constitutional safeguards to prevent discrimination based on language.
